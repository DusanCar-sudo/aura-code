# NASA's Founders: Names, Roles, and Short Histories

## 1. What Does "Founder" Mean for a Government Agency?

NASA was not founded by a private group or a single visionary, but created by an act of Congress — the **National Aeronautics and Space Act of 1958** (Public Law 85-568), signed into law by President Dwight D. Eisenhower on **July 29, 1958**.[1] It began operations on **October 1, 1958**, absorbing the 46-year-old National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA) and its 8,000 employees, three major laboratories, and a $100 million budget.[1]

"Founders" therefore refers not to a start-up team but to a constellation of political leaders, scientists, engineers, and legislative architects who each played an indispensable role in transforming the post-Sputnik crisis into a permanent civilian space agency. This report identifies each key individual by name, with their role and a short history.

## 2. Political and Executive Architects

### Dwight D. Eisenhower (1890–1969) — 34th President of the United States

**Role:** The political founder and ultimate authority who signed NASA into existence.

**Short history:** In the aftermath of the Soviet Union's launch of Sputnik 1 (October 4, 1957), Eisenhower resisted calls for a crash military space programme. He believed space exploration should be openly civilian — to avoid militarising space and to project American values. On November 7, 1957, he appointed Dr. James Killian as the first-ever Special Assistant for Science and Technology. On April 2, 1958, Eisenhower delivered a message to Congress calling for a civilian National Aeronautics and Space Agency built upon the existing NACA. He signed the National Aeronautics and Space Act on July 29, 1958. On August 8, he nominated T. Keith Glennan as NASA's first Administrator and Hugh Dryden as Deputy Administrator.[2][3]

### Senator Lyndon B. Johnson (1908–1973) — Senate Majority Leader, later 36th President

**Role:** The dominant legislative force driving NASA's creation through Congress.

**Short history:** In November 1957, as Senate Majority Leader, Johnson launched the Senate Preparedness Subcommittee hearings that kept missile and space issues in the public eye for weeks. On February 6, 1958, the Senate formed an ad hoc Special Committee on Space and Astronautics with Johnson as chair. He co-introduced the Senate version of the NASA bill (S-3609) with Republican Styles Bridges on April 14, 1958. When the House and Senate versions of the bill conflicted over whether NASA should have a strong policy board or a weak advisory committee, Johnson chaired a 19-member bipartisan panel that produced the compromise. Johnson personally met with Eisenhower at the White House on July 7, 1958, to break the deadlock — suggesting the President himself chair the new National Aeronautics and Space Council — which Eisenhower accepted.[3]

### Congressman John W. McCormack (1891–1980) — House Majority Leader

**Role:** Championed NASA's creation in the House of Representatives.

**Short history:** McCormack chaired the House ad hoc Select Committee on Astronautics and Space Exploration (established March 5, 1958) and introduced the House version of the NASA bill (HR-11881). He was an early and consistent advocate for a unified civilian space effort.[3]

### Senator Styles Bridges (1898–1961) — Republican Senator, New Hampshire

**Role:** Co-sponsor of the Senate NASA bill, providing essential bipartisan cover.

**Short history:** Bridges co-introduced S-3609 with Lyndon Johnson on April 14, 1958. His Republican co-sponsorship was critical to demonstrating that the space programme was a national, not partisan, priority.[3]

### Nelson Rockefeller (1908–1979) — Chairman, President's Advisory Committee on Government Organization

**Role:** Recommended the NACA-based structure that became NASA.

**Short history:** On March 5, 1958, Rockefeller, together with James Killian and Bureau of the Budget Director Percival Brundage, formally recommended to Eisenhower that "leadership of the civil space effort be lodged in a strengthened and redesignated National Advisory Committee for Astronautics." Eisenhower immediately authorised this proposal.[3]

## 3. Scientific and Institutional Architects

### Dr. James R. Killian Jr. (1904–1988) — President of MIT; Eisenhower's Special Assistant for Science and Technology

**Role:** The intellectual architect of the civilian space agency concept.

**Short history:** Appointed by Eisenhower on November 7, 1957, in direct response to Sputnik, Killian chaired the newly constituted President's Science Advisory Committee (PSAC). PSAC's seminal December 1957 report, "Introduction to Outer Space," strongly advocated for a new civilian agency. On December 30, 1957, Killian wrote a memorandum to Eisenhower noting that many scientists held "deeply felt convictions" opposing Defence Department control of space. On March 5, 1958, he co-signed the recommendation that created NASA's NACA-based structure. After leaving the White House in 1959, Killian returned to MIT and continued to advise on science policy.[4]

### Hugh Latimer Dryden (1898–1965) — Director of NACA; First NASA Deputy Administrator

**Role:** The institutional bridge from NACA to NASA and its first Deputy Administrator.

**Short history:** Dryden earned his Ph.D. in physics from Johns Hopkins at age 20 — the youngest doctorate in the university's history. He joined the National Bureau of Standards in 1918 and became NACA's Director of Aeronautical Research in 1946, then Director of NACA in 1947. On January 14, 1958, Dryden published the pivotal position paper "A National Research Program for Space Technology," arguing that "it is of great urgency and importance to our country … that this challenge [Sputnik] be met by an energetic program of research and development for the conquest of space" and that NACA "is capable, by rapid extension and expansion of its effort, of providing leadership in space technology." Eisenhower nominated him as NASA's first Deputy Administrator on August 8, 1958. Dryden served in that role until his death in 1965. According to Tom Wolfe, Dryden was the individual who, in an April 1961 meeting with President Kennedy, first suggested that a crewed Moon landing was the way to "catch up" with the Soviets — leading directly to the Apollo programme.[5][3]

### James H. "Jimmy" Doolittle (1896–1993) — Chairman of NACA

**Role:** Chairman of NACA during the transition, key Congressional witness.

**Short history:** A legendary aviation pioneer (first instrument flight, first US doctorate in aeronautics, Medal of Honor recipient for the 1942 Doolittle Raid on Tokyo), Doolittle was appointed Chairman of NACA's main committee. On May 5, 1958, he testified before the House Committee on Astronautics and Space Exploration that the US civilian space programme had "two focused objectives — gaining scientific data using automated probes and sending into space craft that will carry men on voyages of exploration." He called an early Moon shot a "stunt," arguing for methodical, long-term planning. Doolittle also served on the newly reconstituted PSAC in December 1957 and was among Eisenhower's appointees to the first National Aeronautics and Space Council.[3]

### T. Keith Glennan (1905–1995) — First NASA Administrator

**Role:** NASA's first operational leader, who built the agency from NACA's foundations.

**Short history:** Born in Enderlin, North Dakota, Glennan earned a degree in electrical engineering from Yale (1927) and worked in the motion picture industry before joining Columbia University's Division of War Research in WWII, where he directed the Navy's Underwater Sound Laboratories. In 1947 he became president of the Case Institute of Technology in Cleveland. On August 8, 1958, Eisenhower nominated him as NASA's first Administrator; he was sworn in on August 20. During his tenure (1958–1961), Glennan absorbed the Naval Research Laboratory to create the Goddard Space Flight Center, acquired the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (December 1958), and transferred the Army Ballistic Missile Agency team into the new Marshall Space Flight Center (1960). By mid-1960, Glennan had secured NASA primacy over all federal space activities except reconnaissance satellites and ballistic missiles.[6]

### Dr. Guyford Stever (1916–2010)

**Role:** Headed NACA's Special Committee on Space Technology, which defined the new agency's scope.

**Short history:** On January 12, 1958, NACA created a "Special Committee on Space Technology" headed by Stever. His committee included consultation with Wernher von Braun's Working Group on Vehicular Programs and produced the blueprint for what a non-military space agency should encompass. In October 1958, Stever's committee published its final report, severely criticising the duplication of efforts across US space programmes and laying out a coordinated vision.[1]

### Eilene Galloway (1906–2009) — Senior Analyst, Senate Special Committee on Space and Astronautics

**Role:** Legislative drafter often called "the Grandmother of NASA."

**Short history:** Galloway worked as a senior analyst for Senator Lyndon Johnson's Special Committee on Space and Astronautics. She played a pivotal role in drafting the actual language of the National Aeronautics and Space Act of 1958, helping to resolve differences between the House and Senate versions. Her work ensured that the Act included provisions for international cooperation and the peaceful uses of outer space. She later served on the National Aeronautics and Space Council staff and became a leading scholar of space law and policy.[7]

## 4. Technical and Engineering Pioneers

### Dr. Wernher von Braun (1912–1977) — Director, Army Ballistic Missile Agency

**Role:** The rocket engineer whose team launched America's first satellite and later formed NASA's propulsion core.

**Short history:** A German aerospace engineer who developed the V-2 rocket for Nazi Germany during WWII, von Braun was brought to the United States in 1945 under Operation Paperclip. At the Army Ballistic Missile Agency (ABMA) in Huntsville, Alabama, his team developed the Jupiter-C rocket. After the Soviet Sputnik and the US Vanguard failure, von Braun's team launched Explorer I — America's first satellite — on January 31, 1958, carrying James Van Allen's cosmic ray detector that discovered the Van Allen radiation belts. In January 1958, von Braun headed the "Working Group on Vehicular Programs," which provided the Stever Committee with analysis of the large booster programmes needed for space exploration. In 1960, Glennan transferred von Braun's entire ABMA team into NASA, creating the Marshall Space Flight Center, where von Braun's Saturn V rocket would later take Apollo astronauts to the Moon.[1][3]

## 6. Bottom Line

NASA was not the creation of any single individual but the product of a deliberate, bipartisan political process forged in the crisis of Sputnik. **President Dwight D. Eisenhower** was the political founder who insisted on civilian control. **Senator Lyndon B. Johnson** was the legislative engine who drove the bill through Congress. **James Killian** and **Hugh Dryden** provided the intellectual and institutional blueprint — Dryden's January 1958 paper and Killian's PSAC were decisive in shaping the NACA-based structure. **T. Keith Glennan** was the first administrator who built the operational agency. **Eilene Galloway** drafted the law's language. **Wernher von Braun** provided the rocket technology that gave NASA its early credibility. And **James Doolittle**, **Guyford Stever**, **Nelson Rockefeller**, **John McCormack**, and **Styles Bridges** each provided essential leadership, expertise, and political support at critical moments. The claim sometimes encountered online that Walt Disney or other celebrities "founded NASA" is false; a Reuters fact check in May 2024 explicitly debunked this.[8]
