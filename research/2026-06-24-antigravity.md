# Antigravity: A Comprehensive Research Report

---

## 1. What Is "Antigravity" — Definition and Scope

**Antigravity** is the hypothetical concept of a force, field, or mechanism that would exactly oppose or cancel the force of gravity. More broadly, the term is sometimes used for any means of modifying, shielding, or counteracting gravitational effects without using conventional forces such as aerodynamic lift, electromagnetic levitation, or rocket thrust (Thompson, 2003).

It is crucial to distinguish antigravity from phenomena that are *not* antigravity:
- **Free fall or orbit** — objects in orbit are still under gravity's pull; they are simply falling continuously.
- **Balancing forces** — magnetic levitation (maglev), ion-propelled "lifters," aerodynamic lift, and buoyancy all oppose gravity via known forces, not by modifying gravity itself.
- **Electromagnetic "lifters"** — devices using high-voltage ion wind to push air downward do not function in a vacuum and are not antigravity (Tajmar, 2004).

The concept appears extensively in science fiction, from Francis Godwin's *The Man in the Moone* (1638) to George Tucker's *A Voyage to the Moon* (1827), the latter being the first to treat antigravity from a scientific rather than supernatural angle (Sudbery, Langford & Nicholls, 2020).

## 2. What Does Established Physics Say?

### 2.1 General Relativity

Under the known laws of physics — specifically Einstein's general relativity (GR) — **antigravity is not possible except under highly contrived circumstances** (Peskin & Schroeder, 1995; Wald, 1984; Polchinski, 1998). In GR, the "charge" of gravity is mass-energy, a quantity believed to always be positive. Gravity is universally attractive; there is no "negative gravitational charge" analogous to the unlike charges in electromagnetism that enables repulsion (Nieto & Goldman, 1991).

The Einstein field equations,
\[
G_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}
\]
couple spacetime curvature (the Einstein tensor \(G_{\mu\nu}\)) to the energy-momentum tensor \(T_{\mu\nu}\) of matter. Since the energy density of ordinary matter is always positive, the resulting curvature is always "inward" — i.e., attractive.

### 2.2 Negative Mass in Theory

Hermann Bondi (1957) explored negative mass as a formal possibility within GR. His analysis showed that if negative mass existed, it would lead to paradoxical "runaway motion": a positive mass would attract a negative mass, but the negative mass would repel the positive mass, causing both to accelerate indefinitely in the same direction — a violation of energy conservation. No observational evidence supports the existence of negative mass.

### 2.3 The Antimatter Question

When Paul Dirac predicted antimatter in 1928, a question naturally arose: could antimatter experience *gravitational repulsion* from ordinary matter? Three definitive theoretical arguments were published against this:

1. **Morrison (1958)**: Repulsive antimatter gravity would violate energy conservation in Earth's gravitational field.
2. **Schiff (1959)**: In quantum field theory, virtual electron-positron pairs would break the equivalence of inertial and gravitational mass, contradicting the high-precision Eötvös experiment.
3. **Good (1961)**: The long-lived neutral K-meson (\(K^0_2\)) is a superposition of particle and antiparticle states. If these responded differently to gravity, the particle's decay rate would be affected in measurable ways not observed.

The **ALPHA-g experiment at CERN** (Anderson et al., *Nature*, 2023) delivered the decisive empirical test. By measuring whether antihydrogen atoms fell upward or downward in a vertical vacuum chamber, the collaboration found that antihydrogen falls with an acceleration \(a_{\bar{H}} = (0.75 \pm 0.13~\text{(stat.+syst.)} \pm 0.16~\text{(simulation)}) \, g\), **fully consistent with normal gravity** (\(a = g\)) and ruling out repulsive gravity at the level tested.

### 2.4 Dark Energy: A Different Kind of Repulsion

The 1998 discovery that the expansion of the universe is accelerating — for which Perlmutter, Riess, and Schmidt received the 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics — revealed a form of gravitational repulsion at cosmological scales. This is attributed to **dark energy**, represented in the Einstein field equations by the cosmological constant \(\Lambda\):
\[
G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}
\]

A positive \(\Lambda\) acts as a uniform *repulsive* pressure permeating all space. However, this is **not antigravity** in the conventional sense — it does not cancel or shield local gravity, does not allow levitation of objects, and cannot be harnessed for propulsion. It is a property of spacetime itself at the largest scales, where the energy density of matter has thinned enough for \(\Lambda\) to dominate.

## 3. Experimental Claims and Their Fates

### 3.1 Thomas Townsend Brown and "Electrogravitics" (1920s–)

Brown claimed that high-voltage capacitors exhibited thrust, coining the term "Biefeld–Brown effect." His devices became a staple of UFO and conspiracy-theory literature, with claims they power the B-2 bomber.

**Rigorous follow-ups:**
- **Talley (1990)**, US Air Force study: measured forces with 19 kV in high vacuum; no force above the \(2 \times 10^{-9}\) N detection limit.
- **Campbell (2003)**, NASA: no effect in vacuum.
- **Tajmar et al. (2004, 2024)**, comprehensive search with steady electric fields: **no effect in vacuum**.

**Conclusion**: The observed motion was **ion wind** — a well-understood electrohydrodynamic effect. No antigravity.

### 3.2 Eugene Podkletnov and Rotating Superconductors (1992)

Podkletnov claimed that a rapidly rotating YBCO superconductor reduced the weight of objects placed above it by up to 2%. This generated enormous interest.

**Reproduction attempts** by Li et al. (1997), Woods et al. (2001), Hathaway et al. (2003), and Tajmar & de Matos (2003) **universally failed to reproduce the effect**. The Göde Scientific Foundation's Institute for Gravity Research, which offers a **€1 million prize** for a reproducible antigravity experiment, has also been unable to replicate any such claim.

### 3.3 Gyroscopic "Weight Loss" Claims

Hayasaka & Takeuchi (1989) reported anomalous weight reduction in right-spinning gyroscopes. **Nitschke & Wilmath (1990)** tested the claim and obtained a **null result**. No gyroscopic antigravity device has ever been demonstrated under controlled conditions.

### 3.4 The Gravity Research Foundation

Founded by Roger Babson in 1948 with the initial (somewhat eccentric) goal of developing gravity shields, the Foundation later pivoted to funding serious gravitational research through its essay competition. Winners include **George F. Smoot** (1993, Nobel Prize 2006) and **Gerard 't Hooft** (2015, Nobel Prize 1999). This trajectory mirrors the broader arc of antigravity research: from fanciful engineering ambition to rigorous physical understanding.

### 3.5 NASA Breakthrough Propulsion Physics Program (1996–2002)

NASA funded the BPP program to evaluate exotic propulsion concepts. Antigravity-like ideas were placed in the category of **"approaches categorized as non-viable"** after finding no evidence of antigravity-like forces (Millis, 2005). The program received so many inappropriate proposals that NASA developed a screening guide for reviewers (Millis & Thomas, 2006).

## 4. Theoretical Frontiers: CPT Gravity and Matter-Antimatter Repulsion

Despite the ALPHA-g result, theoretical work continues on whether antimatter might exhibit *subtly* different gravitational behaviour. **Villata (2011)** proposed that if GR is invariant under CPT (charge-parity-time) transformations, matter and antimatter would necessarily repel gravitationally — but this is a framework extension, not standard GR.

**Menary (2025, arXiv:2501.10589)**, building on Villata's work, proposed a "neumatter" scenario where fundamental fermions and antifermions repel, but intermediate vector bosons (IVBs, e.g. photons, gluons) are attracted to both. This yields a prediction that antihydrogen's free-fall acceleration would be \(a_{\bar{H}} \approx 0.78\,g\) — which is **still consistent at ~1σ with the ALPHA-g result** of \(0.75 \pm 0.13\,g\). This demonstrates that while outright repulsion (\(a_{\bar{H}} = -g\)) is ruled out, small deviations from \(g\) are not yet excluded at high precision.

**Crucially**, even in these speculative models, the gravitational mass of antimatter remains positive — there is no "negative mass" and no antigravity in the sense of levitation or gravity cancellation.

## 5. Open Debates and Unknowns

| Question | Status |
|----------|--------|
| Does antimatter fall at exactly \(g\)? | Consistent with \(g\) at ~1σ; higher-precision tests (AEgIS, GBAR at CERN) are in progress |
| Could dark energy be harnessed locally? | No — it is a property of spacetime at cosmological scales; its energy density is ~\(10^{-9}\) J/m³, utterly negligible locally |
| Is negative mass physically possible? | No evidence; would violate energy conditions and create causality paradoxes |
| Could quantum gravity enable antigravity? | Speculative; no working theory of quantum gravity exists |
| Have any antigravity devices ever been replicated? | **No.** The €1M Göde prize remains unclaimed |

## 6. Practical Takeaways

1. **Antigravity as popularly conceived — levitation, gravity shielding, "anti-gravity boots" — is not possible under known physics.** Every claimed demonstration has been traced to mundane effects (ion wind, experimental error) or could not be reproduced.

2. **Antimatter does not exhibit gravitational repulsion.** The ALPHA-g experiment at CERN (2023) confirmed that antihydrogen falls down, not up. Any remaining uncertainty is about whether the acceleration is *exactly* \(g\) or slightly less — not about levitation.

3. **Dark energy is a real form of gravitational repulsion, but at cosmological scales only.** It explains why the universe's expansion is accelerating but is not transferable to local engineering.

4. **The distinction between proven physics and speculative claims is critical.** The history of antigravity is littered with devices that "worked" only in air, only in the presence of their inventors, or only until independent testing occurred. Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence — and in over a century, none has been provided.

5. **For practical applications requiring "anti-gravity-like" effects**, engineers rely on well-understood technologies: magnetic levitation, aerodynamic lift, ion thrusters (in space), tethers, and reaction wheels. These do not modify gravity — they counter it with other forces.

---

## Bottom Line

**Antigravity — a force that cancels, shields, or reverses gravity — does not exist under the known laws of physics.** General relativity requires gravity to be universally attractive for positive mass-energy. Every experimental claim of antigravity has either been debunked by rigorous reproduction attempts or traced to known non-gravitational effects. The 2023 ALPHA-g result at CERN confirmed that even antimatter falls downward. Dark energy provides cosmological-scale repulsion but is irrelevant to local antigravity. The field remains a staple of science fiction and a cautionary tale about the gap between wishful thinking and empirical verification.
